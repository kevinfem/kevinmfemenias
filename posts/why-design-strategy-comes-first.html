UX Strategy Process 15 March 2025

Why design strategy comes before pixels

The instinct to jump straight into Figma is strong. Here's why slowing down to define the problem first leads to better outcomes every time.

Every designer knows the feeling. A brief lands in your inbox, the kickoff meeting wraps up, and there's an almost magnetic pull toward opening Figma. You want to make something. You want to show progress. You want to feel like you're solving the problem.

But jumping straight to pixels is one of the most reliable ways to waste everyone's time — including your own. After eight years of doing this work, I've come to believe that the quality of a design outcome is almost entirely determined before a single frame is drawn.

The problem with starting in Figma

When you open the design tool first, you implicitly accept a set of assumptions. You assume you understand the user's actual problem. You assume the brief is correct. You assume the scope is right. You assume that a visual solution is even the right kind of solution.

These assumptions are almost never all true.

A polished design built on a wrong assumption is just a well-dressed mistake.

I've seen this play out dozens of times. A team spends two weeks designing a new onboarding flow, only to discover in usability testing that users weren't confused by onboarding at all — they were confused by a single label deep inside the product. All that effort, beautifully executed and entirely misdirected.

What strategy actually means

Design strategy isn't a deliverable. It's not a strategy document or a workshop output or a slide deck. It's a way of approaching a problem before committing to a solution.

At its core, it's just a handful of questions:

  • What is the actual problem we're solving — not the problem as stated, but the underlying need?
  • Who specifically has this problem, and what do we know about their context?
  • What does success look like, and how will we know when we've achieved it?
  • What constraints are we designing within — technical, organisational, time?
  • What's the simplest intervention that could meaningfully move the needle?

These questions don't take long to answer. A focused conversation with the right stakeholders — maybe 90 minutes — is usually enough. But skipping them means building your entire design process on an unstable foundation.

Strategy makes execution faster, not slower

The counterintuitive truth is that taking time to do strategy upfront almost always speeds up the overall process. When everyone — designers, PMs, engineers, stakeholders — is aligned on what problem you're solving and what success looks like, decisions get easier. You stop debating details that don't matter yet. You stop going back to stakeholders for direction on questions that should have been settled at the start.

The projects I've worked on that moved fastest were always the ones where we'd done the most thinking before touching the tools.

How I apply this in practice

Before starting any significant design project, I try to get clarity on three things:

  1. The real problem. Not the stated problem, but the underlying one. This usually requires at least one conversation with actual users, not just stakeholders.
  2. The success metric. What number moves, or what behaviour changes, if we get this right? If nobody can answer this, the brief isn't ready.
  3. The constraints. What are we definitely not doing? What's already decided? Knowing the edges of the design space saves enormous amounts of time.

With those three things clear, I can open Figma with confidence — knowing that whatever I make is at least pointed in the right direction.

Design is problem solving. The tools we use are just how we express solutions. Getting the problem right first isn't slowing down — it's doing the most important part of the work.
